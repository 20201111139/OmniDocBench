# Materials and methods  

# Soil sampling and chemical analysis  

After removal of the above-ground plant debris soil samples were taken at Numata farm (  $36^{\circ}36^{\prime}\mathrm{N}$   $139^{\circ}00^{\prime}\mathrm{E}$  altitude  $780{-}810\;\mathrm{~m~}$  , Chiba University, from a typical Japanese cedar forest (Cryptomeria japonica, 40 years old) and its adjacent orchard dominated by apple trees (Malus pumila, 15 years old) in November 2002. The typical forest  land lies  $200\,\textrm{m}$  above the orchard. The surface slopes of both sites are within the range of  $7-12^{\circ}$  Three different soil layers, based on the soils’ attributes, were sampled from 0 to 26,26 to 51,and 51 to  $75\ \mathrm{cm}$  depth under the coniferous forest, and from 0 to 9, 9 to 23, and 23 to  $51\;\;\mathrm{cm}$   under the orchard, respectively. Fresh soils, classified as andisol (FAO soil classification), were sieved  $\left(2\,\textrm{m m}\right)$  to remove small stones and roots in the laboratory and stored in the dark below   $5\mathrm{{}^{\circ}C}$   prior to incubation.  $\mathrm{NH_{4}}^{+}\mathrm{-N}$   and   $\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{\mathrm{~-}}\!\mathrm{N}$   in the soils were extracted with  $1\mathrm{~M~KCl}$   solution. The slurry was shaken for  $30\ \mathrm{min}$  before filtering. The  $\mathrm{NH_{4}}^{+}\mathrm{-N}$   and  $\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{\phantom{\,}}\overline{{\phantom{\,}}}$  -N in the soil extract was measured colorimetrically via the nitroprusside and hydrazine-reduction methods, respectively  $(\mathrm{Kim\1995})$  0. Soil water content was measured by drying the soil at  $105\mathrm{{}^{\circ}C}$  for  $24~\mathrm{h}$  . The total soil C and  $_\mathrm{N}$  contents were measured using a CN analyzer (MT-700 with an Auto Sampler MTA-600; Yanaco, Japan), via formation of gaseous oxides after dry combustion. Fresh soil  $\mathsf{p H}$  (soil/water, 1:2.5) was measured using a portable pH meter. The main soil characteristics are summarized in Table 1. Some surface forest soils (  $\mathrm{[0-5\cm}$  depth) from the same farm were also taken to study the effect of dicyandiamide addition alone  on   $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$  uptake  in soil; their main attributes were measured as mentioned earlier and are shown in Table 2.  

# Soil  $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$  uptake capacity at two initial  $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$  concentrations  

The soil moisture was adjusted to approximately  $45\%$  after  $5.0\mathrm{-g}$   samples of fresh soil (in triplicate) were added to  $100{-}\mathrm{m}1$  serumbottleswhichweresealedwith butyl rubber stoppers. Some wet soil samples from the lower mineral layers were allowed a period of air-drying to standardize their moisture content (approximately   $45\%$  ）before the incubation. Through injecting a series of standard   $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$  volumes  with  a concentration of  $996.1\;\;\;\upmu\mathrm{l}\;\;\;\mathrm{l}^{-1}\;\;\mathrm{CH}_{4}^{\cdot}$  (Takachiho Industrial, Japan) into each bottle, the headspace  $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$   concentrations prior to the incubation were approximately  2.4  and  $12.6~\mathrm{\\upmul~\mathrm{~l~}^{-1}~\mathrm{~CH}_{4}}$  ,respectively. Finally the incubation was carried out at  $25\mathrm{{^\circC}}$  in the dark. At regular intervals  $0.5{\-}\mathrm{ml}$   headspace gases were taken and immediately injected into a gas chromatograph (GC) for  $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$   measurement. Control bottles containing no soil were included to show that the rubber stoppers produced no  measurable  $\mathrm{CH_{4}}$   under  any  of  the  experimental conditions.
