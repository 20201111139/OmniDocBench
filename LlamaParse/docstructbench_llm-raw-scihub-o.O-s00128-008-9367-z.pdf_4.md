Hazard Index (HI from the US EPA's IRIS database (US EPA 2004) The sum of all the HQs for each element is referred to as the Hazard Index (HI). The formulas are as follows: HI < 1: no adverse human health effects are expected to occur; HI ≥ 1 there is moderate or high risk of adverse human effects occurring. Table 3 shows our assessment of health risk: The analysis found that the HI for daily intakes of 1.25 L of green tea, oolong tea, and black tea was 1.3 x 10-1, 2.4 x 10-1, 6.7 x 10-1 respectively, showing the health risk is significantly (ca. 100-fold) lower for green tea consumption than for oolong tea or black tea. However, for frequent consumption of black tea: HI was close to 1.0, suggesting that black tea consumption is likely to have adverse effects. To safeguard the health of the general public, black tea should continue to be monitored. Although there may be health risks from tea consumption, this study indicated that teas still contain many trace elements, such as Zn, Se, and Co, that are essential for maintaining and improving human health. The zinc and selenium contained superoxide dismutase (SOD) act as antioxidants, reducing oxidative damage to cellular DNAs; while enhancing the function of the immune system (Riikaguer et al. 2001). According to Azin et al. (1998), Zn and Se are effective in preventing breast cancer and esophageal cancer: Cobalt is a constituent of vitamin B12. Its deficiency causes impairment of the central nervous system, impairment of neurotransmission, and decline of the immune function (Vellema et al. 1996).
