To avoid nitrification of added $\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}$in the soils and the effects of the associated counter-ions (e.g., $\mathrm{SO}_{4}{ }^{2-}, \mathrm{Cl}^{-1}$ ), we used urea-N together with a nitrification inhibitor, dicyandiamide, which can build up high levels of $\mathrm{NH}_{4}{ }^{+}-\mathrm{N}$ accumulation with little $\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-}-\mathrm{N}$. This allowed us to compare the inhibition by $\mathrm{NH}_{4}{ }^{+}-\mathrm{N}$ with that by $\mathrm{NO}_{3}{ }^{-}-\mathrm{N}$ of the soil $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ uptake. We also studied the effects of dicyandiamide addition alone on the $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ oxidation in surface forest soils.

Materials and methods
Soil sampling and chemical analysis
After removal of the above-ground plant debris soil samples were taken at Numata farm ( $36^{\circ} 36^{\prime} \mathrm{N}, 139^{\circ} 00^{\prime} \mathrm{E}$, altitude 780-810 m), Chiba University, from a typical Japanese cedar forest (Cryptomeria japonica, 40 years old) and its adjacent orchard dominated by apple trees (Malus pumila, 15 years old) in November 2002. The typical forest land lies 200 m above the orchard. The surface slopes of both sites are within the range of $7-12^{\circ}$. Three different soil layers, based on the soils' attributes, were sampled from 0 to 26,26 to 51 , and 51 to 75 cm depth under the coniferous forest, and from 0 to 9,9 to 23 , and 23 to 51 cm under the orchard, respectively. Fresh soils, classified as andisol (FAO soil classification), were sieved $(2 \mathrm{~mm})$ to remove small stones and roots in the laboratory and stored in the dark below $5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ prior to incubation. $\mathrm{NH}_{4}{ }^{+}-\mathrm{N}$ and $\mathrm{NO}_{3}{ }^{-}-\mathrm{N}$ in the soils were extracted with 1 M KCl solution. The slurry was shaken for 30 min before filtering. The $\mathrm{NH}_{4}{ }^{+}-\mathrm{N}$ and $\mathrm{NO}_{3}{ }^{-}-\mathrm{N}$ in the soil extract was measured colorimetrically via the nitroprusside and hydrazine-reduction methods, respectively (Kim 1995). Soil $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ uptake capacity at two initial $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ concentrations

The soil moisture was adjusted to approximately $45 \%$ after $5.0-\mathrm{g}$ samples of fresh soil (in triplicate) were added to $100-\mathrm{ml}$ serum bottles which were sealed with butyl rubber stoppers. Some wet soil samples from the lower mineral layers were allowed a period of air-drying to standardize their moisture content (approximately 45\%) before the incubation. Through injecting a series of standard $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ volumes with a concentration of $996.1 ~ \mu l 1^{-1} \mathrm{CH}_{4}$ (Takachiho Industrial, Japan) into each bottle, the headspace $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ concentrations prior to the incubation were approximately 2.4 and $12.6 \mu \mathrm{l} \mathrm{l}^{-1} \mathrm{CH}_{4}$, respectively. Finally the incubation was carried out at $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in the dark. At regular intervals $0.5-\mathrm{ml}$ headspace gases were taken and immediately injected into a gas chromatograph (GC) for $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ measurement. Control bottles containing no soil were included to show that the rubber stoppers produced no measurable $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$ under any of the experimental conditions.
