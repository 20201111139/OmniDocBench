Consistent with the findings of previous studies, our results indicate that phosphorylation of DCX at S332 by JNK is critical for neurite extension and neuronal migration.

Microtubule dynamics is a necessary attribute of the cytoskeleton in the migration of neurons (Gordon-Weeks, 2004). DCX is one of the proteins that confer the unique microtubule properties to neurons, and neurons in early development have high levels of DCX expression (Gleeson et al., 1999). Phosphorylation of DCX by JNK may be a phosphorylation event that modulates this dynamic regulation in migrating neurons. Previous reports have shown that in vitro phosphorylation of DCX by JNK affects its affinity to microtubules through an effect on its dynamicity (Tanaka et al., 2004). In the present study, we demonstrated that the phosphorylation between DCX by JNK also weakened the interaction between DCX and tubulin. In addition, the results of our study showed that S332 phosphorylation is important for the binding of DCX to tubulin (Figs. 5B and 8A). Brain homogenates analysis (Figs. 5B–F) showed elevated DCX phosphorylation at S332 (Fig. 2B) and reduced interaction between DCX and tubulin (Fig. 7D). These results indicate that the phosphorylation of DCX by JNK is more critical than phosphorylation of Cdk5 in the mouse brain, and this may be because of the greater number of Cdk5 in the mouse brain, and thus the inhibition of Cdk5 activity in vivo is not as effective as the inhibition of JNK activity. Moreover, elevated DCX phosphorylation at S332 in the IZ, JZ, and E16–16 mouse brain. JNK phosphorylates DCX at four sites in the mouse brain.

In the cerebellar cortex, high levels of DCX phosphorylation at JNK sites were observed in these areas (Gdalyahu et al., 2004 and present study). On the basis of the expression patterns of its activating subunits p35 and p39, it is shown to be high in the IZ-CP and CP (Oshima et al., 2002). The phosphorylation state of DCX by several kinases, including JNK and Cdk5, will dynamically change in different migratory steps in radial migration in the cerebral and cerebellar cortices. Cortical neurons change their morphology from multipolar to bipolar in radial migration (Tabata and Nakajima, 2003; Noctor et al., 2004), and both treatment with DCX RNAi (Bai et al., 2003) and loss of Cdk5 impair this transitional change (Ohshima et al., 2007). Delayed migration of neurons in the IZ-CP was observed in DLK−/− mice.
