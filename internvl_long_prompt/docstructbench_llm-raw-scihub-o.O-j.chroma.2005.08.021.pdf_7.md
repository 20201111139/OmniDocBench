For each temperature of study, the calculated values of \( RT \ln \gamma_{\text{Vg}} \) were plotted against \( \alpha_{\text{L}}^{0.5} \). These details are shown in Fig. 3a-e for C.I. Although the Fowkes data analytical approach has a relatively simple mathematical basis, the results can be rationalised within the objectives of the study undertaken. The data so derived are interpreted on the basis of the Fowkes model and used within the complexity limitations of the systems being investigated. Thus, a good correlation coefficient \( R^2 \) was obtained from each of the linear fits, denoted as \( R^2 \) in each of the plots. The slope of the linear fit, obtained as \( \alpha_{\text{L}} \) in each plot, gives the value of the surface free energy, \( \gamma_{\text{SV}} \), at that temperature. To determine the relationship between these two parameters, the values are shown in Table 6 for C.I. Pigment Red 122.

The dispersive component of the surface free energy decreases as the temperature increases. This follows a logical trend as the weaker the dispersive interaction, the easier it would be to remove the molecules from the surface.

However, there was significant variation in the values of the dispersive component of the surface energy as the temperature increased. Further experimental work is necessary to understand the reason for such variation, although owing to the quality of experimental consistency, it is felt that the variation is indicative of the changing nature of the pigment (steric, chemical) as a function of temperature.
