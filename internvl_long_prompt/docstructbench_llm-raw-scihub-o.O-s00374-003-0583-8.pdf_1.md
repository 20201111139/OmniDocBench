Effects of some dicarboxylic acids on the association of dissolved humic substances

Received: 23 July 2002 / Accepted: 20 January 2003 / Published online: 27 February 2003
© Springer-Verlag 2003

Abstract
Changes in molecular size distributions of four different humic materials were evaluated through high performance size-exclusion chromatography (HPSEC), before and after treating humic solutions with naturally occurring dicarboxylic, oxalic, malonic, succinic and glutaric acids. Chromatograms of dissolved humic substances showed a peak decrease in volume as well as an increase in peak elution when the solution pH was lowered from 7 to 3.5 by addition of dicarboxylic acids before HPSEC analysis. The resulting reduction in molecular size was explained by the formation of smaller-sized associations stabilized by the formation of strong intra- and intermolecular bonds among humic superstructures into smaller-sized associations stabilized by the formation of strong intra- and intermolecular bonds among humic molecules. The extent of hydrogen size variation was in the order: oxalic<malonic<succinic<glutaric acid. Dicarboxylic acids progressively increased the number of C atoms and had a larger degree of protonation, which, in turn, had progressively larger negative charges. This resulted in a repulsion from humic molecules. The less associated with the dicarboxylic acids, the closer the electrophoretic mobility of humic substances to that of the dicarboxylic acids. Weakly bound humic superstructures and produced more extensive conformational rearrangements. Recognition of humic molecules by organic acids exuded in the soil solution by either plant roots or soil microbes may be important in understanding the role of humus in soil-plant interactions.

Keywords Humic substances · Supramolecular associations · Dicarboxylic acids · Self-assembling conformations · Molecular sizes

Introduction
Research findings have shown that different carboxylic acids exuded by roots into the soil solution (Vaughan and Malcolm 1985; Junk 1991; Bar-Yosef 1991; Ohwaki and Hirata 1992; Tyler and Strom 1995). It has been hypothesized that the amphiphilic properties of these organic acids may modify the size of humic substances dissolved in the soil solution and influence root activity (Nardi et al. 1996; Pinton and et al. 2001). Enhanced growth of both roots and shoots has been repeatedly accounted for by certain molecular properties of humic substances which, by interacting with root cells, increase their metabolism and enhance their resistance to stress (Vaughan and Malcolm 1985; Nardi et al. 1996; Varanini and Pinton 2001). While the overall mechanism by which humic substances interact with the plant physiological system is still unclear, results showed that small molecular-size humic components, separated from bulk humus through root-exuded organic acids, can affect plant growth by hormone-like activity (Šmidova 1960; O’Donnell 1973; Piccolo et al. 1992; Nardi et al. 1994, 1996).

Humic substances have been recently described as supramolecular associations rather than macromolecular polymers (Conte and Piccolo 1999; Piccolo 2001, 2002). This model implies that small and heterogeneous humic molecules self-assemble in supramolecular conformations mainly by weak forces such as dispersive interactions (van der Waals, π-π, and CH-π bonds) and hydrogen bonds. These weakly bound conformations have only apparent large molecular dimensions which can be either reversibly, disrupted or solubilized by treatment with a humic acid (HA) solution with acetic acid. Treatment of a humic solution with a small amount of either methanol, HCl or acetic acid before preparative size-exclusion chromatography was found to produce a substantial reduction of the chemical heterogeneity of size separates (Piccolo et al. 2002).
