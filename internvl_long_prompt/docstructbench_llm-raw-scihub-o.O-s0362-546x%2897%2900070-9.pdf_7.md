Looking at the three models in Fig. 6, we can notice that the model b is more compact than the other two. This is mainly due to the big D_D value, i.e., DD=77.2 at the site-B (7-TLNND) and the site-E (14-SGGGG), suggesting that the sites corresponding to 84-D and 177-S come closer. The same conclusion can be obtained by the estimation of an area of triangle made up of the three active sites. The area is calculated with the distance from one amino acid to another in the three active sites that lie at the vertices of the triangle. The results are shown in Table 6. The model b clearly has the smallest area. The results are small in the model c, the DEV values are rather low compared to those of a and b (do not recognize a function). It suggests that we cannot recognize the functional system of trypsin directly from the active sites.

As the size of the model protein is much smaller than trypsin, the simpler DEV pattern can be obtained. It permits the analysis at X=3. In the 13-D corresponding to 176-X in trypsin, 13-D was not caught in this analysis. Then, this 13-D was also replaced by G to make a model d. The results are shown in Table 7 and the structural models are shown in Table 8. The data of the models in Table 8. Three cases with high DD values in Table 7 (d-1, d-2, d-3) are noticed. From their calculated area, the model d-3 is to be the most probable candidate representing the situation around the functional system of trypsin. Two possible structures which can be illustrated for the model d-3-1 and d-3-2, with and without clockwise folding, respectively. We cannot differentiate these two, but the model d-3-1 has the smallest area.

In the site-Bs and the site-Es of the model d-3, the distinguished amino acids are 3-H, 11-D, 14-S and 20-C. The first three amino acids correspond to the active sites of trypsin, 40-H, 84-D and 177-S, respectively. Thus, such a series of study provided conclusive proof that the DEV model is a useful tool to predict the active sites of trypsin.
