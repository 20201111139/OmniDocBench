# Daily streamflow modelling and assessment based on the curve-number technique

Jin-Yong Choi,1* Bernard A. Engel1 and Ha Woo Chung2

1 Department of Agricultural and Biological Engineering, Purdue University, West Lafayette, IN 47907-1146, USA.
2 Division of Biological Resources and Material Engineering, Seoul National University, Suwon, Kyonggi 441-744, South Korea

## Abstract:

A cell-based long-term hydrological model (CELTHYM) that can be integrated with a geographical information system (GIS) was developed to predict continuous stream flow from small agricultural watersheds. The CELTHYM uses a cell-by-cell moisture accounting on a daily basis using the curve number (CN) technique considering soil moisture balance. The model was calibrated and validated using measured runoff data from the WS#1 and WS#3 watersheds of the Seoul National University, Department of Agricultural Engineering, in Hwaseong County, Kyonggi Province, WS#3 watershed is about 85% forest and 11.5% of paddy field. Rice paddy field was considered as a model, passy 54% rice soil balances. The CELTHYM was calibrated for the parameter release rate, K, and soil moisture storage coefficient, STC, and results were compared with the measured runoff data for 1986. The validation results for WS#1 considering all daily stream flow were poor with R2 = 0.66, but considerably, all daily stream results for days without rainfall were statistically significant (R2 = 0.66). Results for WS#3 showed good agreement with observed data for all days, and R2, E, and RMSE were 0.92, 0.91 and 2.23 (mm), respectively, suggesting potential for CELTHYM application to other watersheds. However, the model did not perform well, suggesting that additional study of these components is needed. Copyright © 2002 John Wiley & Sons, Ltd.

## INTRODUCTION:

Water resources development and watershed management require an understanding of hydrological variations owing to changes in watershed characteristics over long-term periods (Bhadruri et al., 2000), and spatial variability of watershed characteristics that affect hydrological phenomena also must be evaluated in heterogeneous land-use watersheds. However, hydrological model operations that reflect long-term watershed changes often have limitations owing to difficulties obtaining measured hydrological data and data quantifying land and soil characteristics. Therefore, simulation of stream flow on a daily basis using a long-term hydrological model that is simple to operate with readily available data is needed.

Continuous models, also called long-term hydrological models or continuous stream flow models, typically are focused on estimating water yield from a watershed. Owing to complications and difficulties related to data preparation and operation, however, these hydrological models largely have been used for daily, 10-day and monthly stream flow models.
