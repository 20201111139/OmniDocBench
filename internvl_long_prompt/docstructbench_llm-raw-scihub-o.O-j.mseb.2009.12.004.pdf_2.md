In this paper we report the multitechnique study of the chemical, structural and morphological aspects of a structured monolayer obtained by means of a combined approach to obtain both compositional and topographical aspects.

In particular, we have studied the chemical nature of the structures of the system has been changed by varying the ratio of the two polymers in the blend and characterising the chemical nature of the obtained structures. This particular system, apart the obvious model potentiality aspects, has relevant application perspectives in the field of the outstanding electrical/mechanical properties expected for ordered PV2VP-metal complexes [8]. Furthermore, as it is known that the transfer process of the structures obtained at the air/water interfaces may affect severely the integrity of the transferred films, due to the inherent extraction geometry and the chemical nature of the subphase, we have employed a particular deposition mode of the LB monolayers, i.e., horizontal pre-sipitation Langmuir-Blodgett method (h-LB) [9]. This method allows the deposition of high quality films by reducing to a minimum the lateral stresses on the films [9]. The paper is aimed to show that the different polymer phases observed for the various blend composition by scanning probe microscopes (SPM) techniques can be effectively analysed using ToF-SIMS chemical mapping techniques and that the morphological features substantially coincide with a specific chemical structure.

2. Experimental

Poly(vinylpyridine) (PV2VP, Mw = 200 kDa, Polysciences, Inc.) and poly(styrene) (PS, Mw = 280 kDa, Sp2, Scientific Polymer Products, Inc.) have been dissolved in chloroform with different relative weight ratios of the two polymers (1/1, 1/5, 1/10, 1/15) but keeping a constant total polymer concentration of 0.5 g/L. These solutions were used for preparation of Langmuir polymer layers at the water/air interface in a PTFE trough (LT-102, MicrotestMachines, Belarus). The floating film was compressed at a rate of 0.05 mm/s and the corresponding isotherms were recorded by means of a Wilhelmy plate. The transfer of the floating film (100 or 150 nm thick) on cleaned silicon (100 or 115 nm/mic) were transferred on cleaned silicon (100 or 115 nm/mic) substrates by means of horizontal precipitation (h-LB) method [9]. Concerning the quality of the film transfer, for h-LB method we cannot define a transfer ratio. (While vertical deposition is usually characterised by measured by the transfer ratio, that is the decrease of the area occupied by the monolayer (held at constant pressure) on the water surface divided by the coated area of the solid substrate, instead in h-LB method, it is not significant to define the transfer ratio because film is held at constant pressure during the deposition.

Si substrates were cleaned either in a H2SO4/H2O2 mixture or by UV-O3 treatment, rinsed in ultra pure (MQ) water and dried in N2 flux before deposition.
