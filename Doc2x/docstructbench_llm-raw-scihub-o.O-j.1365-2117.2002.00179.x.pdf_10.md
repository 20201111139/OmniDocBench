## Assessment of the uncertainty

The results of the analyses of the uncertainty of the field data and related assumptions are shown in Figs 13 and 14. Polygons \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{P}},{\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) , and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{D}} \) in the \( x - y \) plane represent the spatial distribution of any possible \( {x}_{\text{adv }} \) , that was obtained from the data sets measured at sites P, M, and D, respectively. The degree of overlap between polygons is expressed as \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} \) (for overlap between \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{P}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) ) and \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{MD}}} \) (for overlap between \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{D}} \) ) (see Eq. (11)).

There is no overlap between \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{P}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) (i.e. \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} = 0 \) ) when \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} \leq  {0.35}\mathrm{\;{km}} \) and \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} \geq  {1.05}\mathrm{\;{km}} \) . With \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} = {0.40} - {1.00} \) \( \mathrm{{km}} \) , on the other hand, \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{P}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) overlap each other (i.e. \( \left. {0 < {\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} < 1}\right) \) . However, the highest value of \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} \) (at \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} = \) \( {0.40}\mathrm{\;{km}} \) ) is as low as 0.137 . Excluding the cases that \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} = {0.40} - {0.45}\mathrm{\;{km}} \) , the overlap is negligible \( \left( {{\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} \approx  0}\right) \) . On the other hand, the overlap of \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{D}} \) is in a significantly different trend from that between \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{P}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) . There is some overlap between \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{M}} \) and \( {\eta }_{\mathrm{D}}\left( {0 < {\rho }_{\mathrm{{MD}}}}\right) \) when \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} = \) \( {0.20} - {0.90}\mathrm{\;{km}} \) . At any magnitude of \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} \) within this range, \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{MD}}} \) is much larger than that of \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} \) (Fig. 14). The highest values of \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{MD}}}\left( {{0.55} - {0.60}}\right) \) are seen when \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} = {0.50} - \) \( {0.70}\mathrm{\;{km}} \) . With \( {y}_{\mathrm{i}} = {0.45} \pm  {0.10}\mathrm{\;{km}},{\rho }_{\mathrm{{MD}}} \) ranges between 0.31 and 0.55, still much larger than the highest of \( {\rho }_{\mathrm{{PM}}} \) .
